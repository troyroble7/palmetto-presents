Debt Commentary & Possible Outcomes

Scenario 1: Managed Deleveraging (Base Case — 50%)

Sunrun has $823M cash, no recourse maturities until March 2028, and is generating $350-450M/yr cash. They can service the debt and slowly pay down recourse obligations. The $14.7B non-recourse debt is asset-backed and ring-fenced — it doesn't trigger corporate bankruptcy. This is what management is executing: defensive posture, affiliate cuts, margin over volume. They survive but shrink. opportunity: Grab the displaced affiliate volume and prove the pure-play EPC model scales better.

Scenario 2: Strategic Asset Sales / JV Acceleration (30%)

The Hannon Armstrong JV ($500M) and the shift to 50% asset-sale structures in Q4 signal Sunrun may increasingly sell off system portfolios to reduce balance sheet risk. If they accelerate this, Sunrun becomes more of an origination platform and less of an asset owner. This reduces their long-term recurring revenue moat (the VPP/grid services story depends on owning assets). opportunity: Position as the asset OWNER model that retains full customer value over 25 years — the opposite of Sunrun's fire-sale approach.

Scenario 3: Distress / Restructuring (15%)

$1B/year interest on a company guiding $350M cash generation is razor-thin. If interest rates stay elevated, ABS markets tighten, or volume drops further, the math breaks. Altman Z-Score of -0.47 and 46.5% bankruptcy probability aren't theoretical. The $473.7M in convertible notes and $238M revolver are the pressure points — non-recourse debt is ring-fenced but corporate liquidity could get squeezed. A restructuring would likely mean converting debt to equity (diluting shareholders to ~$0) while the solar asset portfolios continue operating under new ownership. opportunity: Massive channel disruption. Sunrun EPCs and customers would need a new TPO home.

Scenario 4: Acquisition Target (5%)

The Bottom Line on Sunrun's Debt

Sunrun is NOT going bankrupt tomorrow. The non-recourse structure protects the corporate entity from $14B of the debt. But they're running a company that pays $1B/year in interest while generating $350M in cash and guiding for declining unit economics. Every quarter they need to securitize more debt just to fund new originations. It's a treadmill. They're choosing margin over growth (cutting 40%+ of affiliates) because they HAVE to, not because they want to. The stock market sees through this — hence the 30%+ crash despite beating EPS by $0.46.
