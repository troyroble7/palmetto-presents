The Future of Solar Finance

What is Solar 3.0?

The solar industry has evolved through three distinct eras. Solar 3.0 represents the shift from customer acquisition to long-term customer value creation.

The Three Eras of Residential Solar

To understand where the industry is going, you need to understand where it's been.

1.0
The Pioneers
2006 – 2015

Cash sales and early loans. Solar was expensive, niche, and driven by environmental early adopters. Financing was limited.

2.0
The Boom
2016 – 2024

TPO and loan products exploded. Customer acquisition at all costs. Volume mattered more than quality. Many finance providers cut corners.

3.0
Long-Term Value
2025 – Forward

The best finance providers are enterprise partners, not just capital providers. Quality, customer experience, and 25-year asset health define the winners.

Why Solar 2.0 Broke

Solar 2.0 was defined by a simple equation: acquire customers as fast as possible, securitize the contracts, repeat. The problem? Nobody was thinking about what happened after the sale.

Let's be clear about where the problem lies: a small minority of bad actors — roughly 10-20% of sales reps and install companies — damaged the industry's reputation for the rest of us. Their overselling, inflated system pricing, and poor installations led to customer complaints, collection issues, ABS downgrades, and more expensive capital for everyone. The majority of EPCs and sales reps do excellent work — but the bad actors dragged the entire industry down.

  • Sunnova filed Chapter 11 in June 2025 — customers report months-long repair delays, and EPCs lost revenue on completed projects
  • Mosaic filed Chapter 11 in June 2025 — ~$130M in unfunded projects stranded consumers and installers alike
  • GoodLeap's legacy loan ABS is being downgraded by all three rating agencies — delinquencies at all-time highs (note: GoodLeap is now primarily a lease company, but the legacy loan book continues to weigh on their balance sheet)
  • Sunrun introduced a 15-year lease — the only TPO to do so — likely because Wall Street wants faster capital return. A 15-year term increases the monthly payment (limiting cost-effective markets), decreases warranty and battery replacement coverage, and shifts risk to the homeowner

The root cause is the same across all of them: growth outpaced quality controls, and EPCs paid the price through payment delays, broken commitments, and rising capital costs. Solar 2.0 treated customers as transactions. Solar 3.0 treats them as 25-year relationships.
